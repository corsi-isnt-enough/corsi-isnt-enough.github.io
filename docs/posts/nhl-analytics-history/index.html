Corsi Isn’t Enough

Published

June 6, 2026

“The goal of analytics is not to replace the eye test — it’s to sharpen it.”

For most of hockey’s history, the box score was the whole story. Goals, assists, plus/minus — these were the instruments we used to judge players and teams. They felt concrete. They felt sufficient.

They weren’t.

Over the last two decades, a small but relentless community of analysts, bloggers, and eventually team employees have rebuilt the statistical foundation of hockey evaluation from the ground up — not once, but four distinct times. Each generation of metrics answered a question the previous generation couldn’t. Each one was eventually exposed as incomplete. And each one made the next generation possible.

This post traces that full arc: from the first Corsi numbers scratched into a Buffalo Sabres notebook in 2007, to the AI-powered tracking models running in every NHL arena today. We’ll cover the math, the data, and — most importantly — what each generation was actually trying to solve.

The Problem That Started Everything: Why Plus/Minus Had to Go

Before we can appreciate what analytics built, we need to understand what it was reacting against. The dominant player evaluation metric for decades was plus/minus (+/−): a simple count of even-strength goals scored and allowed while a player was on the ice.

\[ \text{Plus/Minus} = \text{Goals For}_{5v5, \text{on-ice}} - \text{Goals Against}_{5v5, \text{on-ice}} \]

On the surface, this seems reasonable. A player who’s on the ice for a lot of your goals and not many of your opponents’ goals must be doing something right. The problem is statistical, and it’s fatal: goals are extraordinarily rare events.

A top-line forward playing 20 minutes a night might be on the ice for 60–80 even-strength goal events across an entire season — as both scorer and victim. That’s not a sample size; that’s noise. A player could post a +20 or a −20 in a given season almost entirely due to randomness in when those rare goals happened to occur.

And that’s before you account for:

  • Teammate quality — a defensive zone specialist sheltered by elite offensive linemates will look far better than he is
  • Score effects — teams losing by two goals push forward and take more risks, artificially inflating the opponent’s counting stats
  • Zone deployment — a shutdown pair that never leaves the defensive zone will always look like a liability in raw +/−
  • Competition quality — no adjustment for whether you’re matched against the Bergeron line or the fourth line

The entire history of NHL analytics is the story of trying to solve these four problems, one generation at a time.

Let’s look at just how noisy goal-based metrics really are. Using standings data from 2007 through 2025, we can measure how well a team’s performance in one season predicts their performance the next — the year-over-year correlation. A metric with high predictive stability (r close to 1.0) contains real signal. A metric with low stability is mostly noise.

Generation 1 — Possession Metrics: Corsi & Fenwick (2007–2013)

The Breakthrough Insight

“Goals tell us who scored. Shots tell us who played in the right end of the ice.”

Around 2007, Jim Corsi — then the goaltending coach for the Buffalo Sabres — started tracking something no one had formally quantified before: all shot attempts, not just the ones that hit the net. Goals, shots on goal, missed shots, and blocked shots, all lumped together as a single possession proxy.

His intuition was elegant: if your team is generating shot attempts at the other end and preventing them at your own, you’re controlling the puck. And puck control, over a large enough sample, drives winning.

The Math

Corsi For % (CF%) is the percentage of all shot attempts that happen in your favour while a player or team is on the ice at even strength:

\[ \text{CF\%} = \frac{\text{Corsi For}}{\text{Corsi For} + \text{Corsi Against}} \times 100 \]

Where:

\[ \text{Corsi For} = \text{Goals For} + \text{Shots on Goal For} + \text{Missed Shots For} + \text{Blocked Shots For} \]

A CF% above 50% means you’re generating more shot attempts than you’re allowing — you’re winning the possession battle. Fenwick (FF%) is a close variant that excludes blocked shots on the theory that shot-blocking is a partially independent defensive skill:

\[ \text{FF\%} = \frac{\text{Unblocked Shot Attempts For}}{\text{Unblocked Shot Attempts For} + \text{Unblocked Shot Attempts Against}} \times 100 \]

The two metrics correlate at roughly r = 0.97, so in practice they’re interchangeable. Corsi became the default.

Why It Worked: The Stabilization Argument

The key advantage of Corsi over goal-based metrics is sheer sample size. A team accrues roughly 80 shot attempt events per game — compared to 5–6 even-strength goals. That’s a 13–16× increase in data per game, which means the metric stabilizes (reaches reliable signal-to-noise ratios) far earlier in the season.

np.random.seed(42)

def simulate_stability(true_talent, events_per_season):
    sample_sizes = np.arange(10, events_per_season + 1, 10)
    correlations = []
    for n in sample_sizes:
        half1 = np.random.binomial(n, true_talent.clip(0.01, 0.99)) / n
        half2 = np.random.binomial(n, true_talent.clip(0.01, 0.99)) / n
        r, _ = stats.pearsonr(half1, half2)
        correlations.append(max(0, r))
    return sample_sizes, np.array(correlations)

An Important Refinement: Score Effects

One critical early fix was adjusting for score effects. Teams protecting a lead intentionally sit back and cede possession, while trailing teams push forward and generate more shot attempts. This means raw Corsi is contaminated by game state — a team that’s perpetually winning will look like a worse possession team than it really is.

The fix: calculate Corsi only in close-game situations (within one goal in the first two periods, tied in the third). This “CF% close” or “score-adjusted CF%” became the standard for meaningful comparisons.

The Limits of Generation 1

Corsi dominated analytical discourse from roughly 2010 through 2015. High-CF% teams tended to win more, and the metric genuinely predicted future performance better than any prior stat. But the cracks started showing in the playoffs, where sample sizes shrink and the relationship between Corsi and winning loosens.

Generation 2 — Shot Quality: Expected Goals (xG) (2014–2018)

The Problem Being Solved

“A breakaway and a blue-line slap shot are both worth exactly one Corsi. They are not worth the same thing.”

The fundamental flaw in Corsi is that it treats all shot attempts as equal. A screened wrist shot from the slot — which scores perhaps 18% of the time — counts the same as a point shot through a wall of bodies, which scores maybe 2% of the time. Aggregate enough of these misweighted events and you can fool the metric: a team that generates a massive volume of low-quality shots will look like a possession powerhouse.

Expected Goals (xG) assigns every shot attempt a probability of resulting in a goal, based on its characteristics. The concept migrated into hockey from soccer analytics around 2013–2014, and a wave of independent analysts built early NHL implementations.

The Math

The basic formulation is a logistic regression:

\[ xG = P(\text{goal} \mid \text{shot location, shot type, game situation,} \ldots) \]

In log-odds form:

\[ \log\left(\frac{xG}{1-xG}\right) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot d + \beta_2 \cdot \theta + \beta_3 \cdot \text{IsRebound} + \beta_4 \cdot \text{IsFastbreak} + \ldots \]

Where \(d\) is distance from goal in feet and \(\theta\) is shot angle from centre. Modern implementations (MoneyPuck, Evolving Hockey) use gradient-boosted trees with dozens of features including traffic, prior event type, time since last event, and game state.

def xg_model(distance_ft, angle_deg, is_rebound=False, shot_type='wrist'):
    # Coefficients calibrated against published NHL shot-conversion benchmarks
    # (MoneyPuck / Evolving Hockey public data, 2015-2024)
    b0      = -1.1715
    b_dist  = -0.0286
    b_angle = -0.0242
    b_reb   =  0.400
    b_type  = {'wrist': 0.0, 'snap': 0.350, 'backhand': -0.30,
               'slap': -0.350, 'tip': 0.350, 'deflection': 0.500
              }.get(shot_type, 0)
    logit = b0 + b_dist*distance_ft + b_angle*angle_deg + b_reb*is_rebound + b_type
    return 1 / (1 + np.exp(-logit))

The PDO Insight: Separating Luck from Skill

A key companion metric to xG was PDO (named after the analyst who popularized it), defined as:

\[ \text{PDO} = \text{Shooting \%} + \text{Save \%} \]

The insight: for an average team facing average goaltending, PDO should hover around 100% (or 1.000 in decimal form). Teams with extremely high PDO — say, 103 — are likely getting unusually lucky shooting percentages or unsustainably good goaltending. xG helps isolate which component is real talent versus regression bait.

Key xG-Era Derived Metrics

Metric Formula What it captures
xGF% \(\frac{xGF}{xGF + xGA}\) Shot quality share — possession quality
xG/60 \(\frac{xG}{TOI} \times 60\) Rate-normalized offense or defense
GSAx \(\text{Goals Saved} - xGA\) Goalie performance above expectation
PDO \(\text{Sh\%} + \text{Sv\%}\) Composite luck/sustainability indicator

xGF% became the dominant team and player evaluation metric by around 2016–17, replacing CF% as the go-to for identifying sustainable performance. But a new problem was emerging: even xGF% couldn’t tell you how much of a team’s quality was attributable to any individual player.

Generation 3 — Player Value: WAR, RAPM & GSVA (2018–2022)

The Problem Being Solved

“Corsi tells us about possession. xG tells us about shot quality. Neither tells us how many wins a single player is actually worth.”

By the late 2010s, hockey analytics borrowed the most important conceptual framework in baseball statistics: Wins Above Replacement (WAR). The question it answers is deceptively simple — if you replaced this player with a freely available replacement-level player (a fringe AHL call-up), how many fewer wins would your team have?

Collapsing all contributions into a single number allows direct comparison across positions, roles, and ice-time situations. A 4-WAR forward and a 4-WAR defenseman are equally valuable, even if they contribute in completely different ways.

The Engine: RAPM

The technical core of hockey WAR is Regularized Adjusted Plus/Minus (RAPM) — a ridge-regression model that estimates each player’s individual contribution to on-ice xG share while simultaneously controlling for all of their teammates and opponents.

The model estimates player effects \(\beta_p\) by solving:

\[ xGF\%_{\text{shift}} = \alpha + \sum_{p \in \text{skaters on ice}} \beta_p + \varepsilon \]

With an L2 (ridge) regularization penalty that shrinks uncertain estimates toward zero:

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \left[ \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2 + \lambda \|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2 \right] \]

The \(\lambda\) penalty is critical: it prevents overfitting for players with small sample sizes (fourth-liners, healthy scratches playing spot minutes). Without it, a player who happened to be on the ice for a fluky 3-goal shift in 12 total minutes would look like the best player in the league.

The left panel shows the key insight: the model deliberately shrinks low-TOI players’ estimates more than high-TOI players. This is the correct move — a player with 200 minutes of ice time has a far noisier RAPM estimate than one with 1,400 minutes, and the model reflects that uncertainty by pulling the estimate closer to league average. The right panel shows that at a moderate λ, RAPM does a good job recovering actual underlying talent — not perfect, but meaningfully better than raw observed statistics.

From RAPM to WAR

The Evolving Hockey WAR model (Arsenault & Desjardins, 2019) builds on RAPM by:

  1. Breaking the player effect into components: even-strength offense, even-strength defense, powerplay, penalty kill, penalties drawn/taken, faceoffs
  2. Scaling each component from xG units to Goals Above Replacement (GAR)
  3. Converting goals to wins using a league-wide goals-per-win factor (~5–6 goals per win)

\[ \text{WAR} = \frac{\text{GAR}_{\text{total}} }{\text{Goals Per Win}} \]

For goalies, the equivalent is Goals Saved Above Expected (GSAx):

\[ \text{GSAx} = xGA - GA \]

A goalie with GSAx = +12 saved 12 more goals than an average goalie would have against the same shot profile. At ~5–6 goals per win, that’s roughly 2 wins of goalie value above average.

The WAR Era’s Limits

WAR was a genuine leap — suddenly you could ask “is this defenseman worth his $6M contract?” with actual math behind the answer. But the model remained constrained by what the data could see: everything measured was still shot-attempt derived. A player’s defensive positioning, his off-puck movement, his ability to win board battles and create separation — none of it registered unless it eventually showed up as a shot attempt. The map was better, but the territory was still mostly invisible.

Generation 4 — The Tracking Era (2022–Present)

The Problem Being Solved

“Hockey is 60 minutes of off-puck movement, defensive reads, and positioning decisions. Most of it never becomes a shot attempt.”

The NHL’s partnership with optical player tracking technology — deployed at scale starting around 2021–22 — finally opened the door to the layer of hockey that had always been analytically invisible. Every player’s position, speed, and movement is now tracked multiple times per second, and the puck itself carries a chip that logs its location and velocity.

Zone Entries and Exits: The Bridge Metric

Even before full tracking arrived, manually-tracked zone entry and exit data (pioneered by Corey Sznajder’s All Three Zones project starting around 2013) revealed something fundamental about how offense is actually generated.

What’s Next: The AI Frontier

The most advanced work happening in NHL analytics departments right now — most of it proprietary — is pushing into territory that would have been science fiction a decade ago:

  • Graph neural networks that model all 10 skaters simultaneously as a formation, not as individuals
  • Sequence models (LSTM / Transformer) that compute xG based on the last 10–15 seconds of play, not just the shot itself
  • Off-puck movement valuation using optical flow to assign credit for creating passing lanes and drawing defenders
  • Integrated contract models that predict future performance and cap value from the full metric stack

Conclusion: Why No Single Metric Is Ever Enough

The history of NHL analytics is not a story of one metric replacing another. It’s a story of layering — each generation answering a question the previous one couldn’t ask.

The problem The solution
+/− is too noisy → Corsi: higher sample rate, real signal
Corsi ignores shot quality → xG: probability-weighted shots
xG can’t isolate individual skill → WAR/RAPM: regression-based context adjustment
WAR can’t see off-puck play → Tracking: full player and puck data

The modern analyst doesn’t choose between these generations. They read all four simultaneously, the way a doctor synthesizes blood work, imaging, and clinical observation before making a diagnosis. Each layer confirms, challenges, or contextualizes the others.

Corsi isn’t enough. But it was the insight that started everything — and understanding why it isn’t enough is what makes every metric that came after it meaningful.

> *"The goal of analytics is not to replace the eye test — it's to sharpen it."*

For most of hockey's history, the box score was the whole story. Goals, assists, plus/minus — these were the instruments we used to judge players and teams. They felt concrete. They felt sufficient.

They weren't.

Over the last two decades, a small but relentless community of analysts, bloggers, and eventually team employees have rebuilt the statistical foundation of hockey evaluation from the ground up — not once, but four distinct times. Each generation of metrics answered a question the previous generation couldn't. Each one was eventually exposed as incomplete. And each one made the next generation possible.

This post traces that full arc: from the first Corsi numbers scratched into a Buffalo Sabres notebook in 2007, to the AI-powered tracking models running in every NHL arena today. We'll cover the math, the data, and — most importantly — *what each generation was actually trying to solve*.

---

## The Problem That Started Everything: Why Plus/Minus Had to Go

Before we can appreciate what analytics built, we need to understand what it was reacting against. The dominant player evaluation metric for decades was **plus/minus** (+/−): a simple count of even-strength goals scored and allowed while a player was on the ice.

$$
\text{Plus/Minus} = \text{Goals For}_{5v5, \text{on-ice}} - \text{Goals Against}_{5v5, \text{on-ice}}
$$

On the surface, this seems reasonable. A player who's on the ice for a lot of your goals and not many of your opponents' goals must be doing something right. The problem is statistical, and it's fatal: **goals are extraordinarily rare events.**

A top-line forward playing 20 minutes a night might be on the ice for 60–80 even-strength goal events across an entire season — as both scorer and victim. That's not a sample size; that's noise. A player could post a +20 or a −20 in a given season almost entirely due to randomness in when those rare goals happened to occur.

And that's before you account for:

- **Teammate quality** — a defensive zone specialist sheltered by elite offensive linemates will look far better than he is
- **Score effects** — teams losing by two goals push forward and take more risks, artificially inflating the opponent's counting stats
- **Zone deployment** — a shutdown pair that never leaves the defensive zone will always look like a liability in raw +/−
- **Competition quality** — no adjustment for whether you're matched against the Bergeron line or the fourth line

The entire history of NHL analytics is the story of trying to solve these four problems, one generation at a time.

Let's look at just how noisy goal-based metrics really are. Using standings data from 2007 through 2025, we can measure how well a team's performance in one season predicts their performance the next — the year-over-year correlation. A metric with high predictive stability (r close to 1.0) contains real signal. A metric with low stability is mostly noise.

## Generation 1 — Possession Metrics: Corsi & Fenwick (2007–2013)

### The Breakthrough Insight

> *"Goals tell us who scored. Shots tell us who played in the right end of the ice."*

Around 2007, Jim Corsi — then the goaltending coach for the Buffalo Sabres — started tracking something no one had formally quantified before: **all shot attempts**, not just the ones that hit the net. Goals, shots on goal, missed shots, and blocked shots, all lumped together as a single possession proxy.

His intuition was elegant: if your team is generating shot attempts at the other end and preventing them at your own, you're controlling the puck. And puck control, over a large enough sample, drives winning.

### The Math

**Corsi For % (CF%)** is the percentage of all shot attempts that happen in your favour while a player or team is on the ice at even strength:

$$
\text{CF\%} = \frac{\text{Corsi For}}{\text{Corsi For} + \text{Corsi Against}} \times 100
$$

Where:

$$
\text{Corsi For} = \text{Goals For} + \text{Shots on Goal For} + \text{Missed Shots For} + \text{Blocked Shots For}
$$

A CF% above 50% means you're generating more shot attempts than you're allowing — you're winning the possession battle. **Fenwick (FF%)** is a close variant that excludes blocked shots on the theory that shot-blocking is a partially independent defensive skill:

$$
\text{FF\%} = \frac{\text{Unblocked Shot Attempts For}}{\text{Unblocked Shot Attempts For} + \text{Unblocked Shot Attempts Against}} \times 100
$$

The two metrics correlate at roughly r = 0.97, so in practice they're interchangeable. Corsi became the default.

### Why It Worked: The Stabilization Argument

The key advantage of Corsi over goal-based metrics is sheer **sample size**. A team accrues roughly 80 shot attempt events per game — compared to 5–6 even-strength goals. That's a 13–16× increase in data per game, which means the metric stabilizes (reaches reliable signal-to-noise ratios) far earlier in the season.

```{python}
#| label: fig-stabilization
#| fig-cap: "Metric stabilization curves: how many games before signal exceeds noise? Corsi reaches the r=0.70 reliability threshold in roughly 25–30 games. Goal-based metrics may never stabilize over a single season."

np.random.seed(42)

def simulate_stability(true_talent, events_per_season):
    sample_sizes = np.arange(10, events_per_season + 1, 10)
    correlations = []
    for n in sample_sizes:
        half1 = np.random.binomial(n, true_talent.clip(0.01, 0.99)) / n
        half2 = np.random.binomial(n, true_talent.clip(0.01, 0.99)) / n
        r, _ = stats.pearsonr(half1, half2)
        correlations.append(max(0, r))
    return sample_sizes, np.array(correlations)
```

### An Important Refinement: Score Effects

One critical early fix was adjusting for **score effects**. Teams protecting a lead intentionally sit back and cede possession, while trailing teams push forward and generate more shot attempts. This means raw Corsi is contaminated by game state — a team that's perpetually winning will look like a worse possession team than it really is.

The fix: calculate Corsi only in **close-game situations** (within one goal in the first two periods, tied in the third). This "CF% close" or "score-adjusted CF%" became the standard for meaningful comparisons.

### The Limits of Generation 1

Corsi dominated analytical discourse from roughly 2010 through 2015. High-CF% teams tended to win more, and the metric genuinely predicted future performance better than any prior stat. But the cracks started showing in the playoffs, where sample sizes shrink and the relationship between Corsi and winning loosens.

## Generation 2 — Shot Quality: Expected Goals (xG) (2014–2018)

### The Problem Being Solved

> *"A breakaway and a blue-line slap shot are both worth exactly one Corsi. They are not worth the same thing."*

The fundamental flaw in Corsi is that it treats all shot attempts as equal. A screened wrist shot from the slot — which scores perhaps 18% of the time — counts the same as a point shot through a wall of bodies, which scores maybe 2% of the time. Aggregate enough of these misweighted events and you can fool the metric: a team that generates a massive volume of low-quality shots will look like a possession powerhouse.

**Expected Goals (xG)** assigns every shot attempt a probability of resulting in a goal, based on its characteristics. The concept migrated into hockey from soccer analytics around 2013–2014, and a wave of independent analysts built early NHL implementations.

### The Math

The basic formulation is a logistic regression:

$$
xG = P(\text{goal} \mid \text{shot location, shot type, game situation,} \ldots)
$$

In log-odds form:

$$
\log\left(\frac{xG}{1-xG}\right) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot d + \beta_2 \cdot \theta + \beta_3 \cdot \text{IsRebound} + \beta_4 \cdot \text{IsFastbreak} + \ldots
$$

Where $d$ is distance from goal in feet and $\theta$ is shot angle from centre. Modern implementations (MoneyPuck, Evolving Hockey) use gradient-boosted trees with dozens of features including traffic, prior event type, time since last event, and game state.

```{python}
#| label: fig-xg-model
#| fig-cap: "Left: xG probability by shot location. The slot — roughly the area from the crease to the top of the circles — is where games are won and lost. Right: xG by shot scenario, illustrating the ~10× range from a blue-line slap shot (~3.6%) to a crease deflection (~34.9%)."

def xg_model(distance_ft, angle_deg, is_rebound=False, shot_type='wrist'):
    # Coefficients calibrated against published NHL shot-conversion benchmarks
    # (MoneyPuck / Evolving Hockey public data, 2015-2024)
    b0      = -1.1715
    b_dist  = -0.0286
    b_angle = -0.0242
    b_reb   =  0.400
    b_type  = {'wrist': 0.0, 'snap': 0.350, 'backhand': -0.30,
               'slap': -0.350, 'tip': 0.350, 'deflection': 0.500
              }.get(shot_type, 0)
    logit = b0 + b_dist*distance_ft + b_angle*angle_deg + b_reb*is_rebound + b_type
    return 1 / (1 + np.exp(-logit))
```

### The PDO Insight: Separating Luck from Skill

A key companion metric to xG was **PDO** (named after the analyst who popularized it), defined as:

$$
\text{PDO} = \text{Shooting \%} + \text{Save \%}
$$

The insight: for an average team facing average goaltending, PDO should hover around 100% (or 1.000 in decimal form). Teams with extremely high PDO — say, 103 — are likely getting unusually lucky shooting percentages or unsustainably good goaltending. xG helps isolate *which* component is real talent versus regression bait.

### Key xG-Era Derived Metrics

| Metric | Formula | What it captures |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| **xGF%** | $\frac{xGF}{xGF + xGA}$ | Shot quality share — possession *quality* |
| **xG/60** | $\frac{xG}{TOI} \times 60$ | Rate-normalized offense or defense |
| **GSAx** | $\text{Goals Saved} - xGA$ | Goalie performance above expectation |
| **PDO** | $\text{Sh\%} + \text{Sv\%}$ | Composite luck/sustainability indicator |

xGF% became the dominant team and player evaluation metric by around 2016–17, replacing CF% as the go-to for identifying sustainable performance. But a new problem was emerging: even xGF% couldn't tell you how much of a team's quality was attributable to any *individual* player.

## Generation 3 — Player Value: WAR, RAPM & GSVA (2018–2022)

### The Problem Being Solved

> *"Corsi tells us about possession. xG tells us about shot quality. Neither tells us how many wins a single player is actually worth."*

By the late 2010s, hockey analytics borrowed the most important conceptual framework in baseball statistics: **Wins Above Replacement (WAR)**. The question it answers is deceptively simple — if you replaced this player with a freely available replacement-level player (a fringe AHL call-up), how many fewer wins would your team have?

Collapsing all contributions into a single number allows direct comparison across positions, roles, and ice-time situations. A 4-WAR forward and a 4-WAR defenseman are equally valuable, even if they contribute in completely different ways.

### The Engine: RAPM

The technical core of hockey WAR is **Regularized Adjusted Plus/Minus (RAPM)** — a ridge-regression model that estimates each player's individual contribution to on-ice xG share while simultaneously controlling for all of their teammates and opponents.

The model estimates player effects $\beta_p$ by solving:

$$
xGF\%_{\text{shift}} = \alpha + \sum_{p \in \text{skaters on ice}} \beta_p + \varepsilon
$$

With an L2 (ridge) regularization penalty that shrinks uncertain estimates toward zero:

$$
\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \left[ \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2 + \lambda \|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2 \right]
$$

The $\lambda$ penalty is critical: it prevents overfitting for players with small sample sizes (fourth-liners, healthy scratches playing spot minutes). Without it, a player who happened to be on the ice for a fluky 3-goal shift in 12 total minutes would look like the best player in the league.

```{python}
#| label: fig-rapm
#| fig-cap: "RAPM ridge regularization in action. Left: larger λ values shrink low-TOI players' estimates more aggressively toward zero. Right: with the right λ, RAPM recovers the underlying true talent fairly well — high-TOI players (yellow) cluster tighter around the 45° line."

np.random.seed(42)
n_players = 600
true_rapm    = np.random.normal(0, 1.5, n_players)
toi_minutes  = np.random.lognormal(np.log(600), 0.5, n_players)
noise_scale  = 3000 / toi_minutes
observed     = true_rapm + np.random.normal(0, noise_scale)

ridge_estimates = {}
for lam in [0, 0.5, 2.0, 8.0]:
    shrinkage = 1 / (1 + lam / (toi_minutes / 60))
    ridge_estimates[lam] = observed * shrinkage

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(13, 5))

ax = axes[0]
for lam, col in zip([0.5, 2.0, 8.0], [GREEN, GOLD, NHL_RED]):
    est   = ridge_estimates[lam]
    r_val = np.corrcoef(true_rapm, est)[0,1]
    ax.scatter(toi_minutes/60, np.abs(observed - est), alpha=0.15, s=8, color=col)
    ax.plot([], [], color=col, label=f'λ={lam}  (r={r_val:.3f} vs truth)')
ax.set_xlabel('TOI (hours played)')
ax.set_ylabel('Shrinkage applied |raw − ridge|')
ax.set_title('Ridge Regularization: More Shrinkage for Low-TOI Players', fontsize=11)
ax.legend(fontsize=9)
ax.grid()

ax2 = axes[1]
best_est = ridge_estimates[2.0]
sc = ax2.scatter(true_rapm, best_est, c=toi_minutes/60, cmap='viridis', alpha=0.4, s=15)
plt.colorbar(sc, ax=ax2, label='TOI (hours)')
m, b = np.polyfit(true_rapm, best_est, 1)
xfit = np.linspace(-5, 5, 100)
ax2.plot(xfit, m*xfit+b, color=ORANGE, lw=2)
ax2.plot(xfit, xfit, '--', color='#8b949e', lw=1.2, alpha=0.6)
r_best = np.corrcoef(true_rapm, best_est)[0,1]
ax2.set_xlabel('True RAPM talent')
ax2.set_ylabel('Estimated RAPM (λ=2.0)')
ax2.set_title(f'RAPM Recovery of True Talent (r={r_best:.3f})\nColored by TOI — high TOI = tighter estimates', fontsize=11)
ax2.grid()

plt.suptitle('The RAPM Engine: Ridge Regression Inside WAR', fontsize=13, y=1.01, fontweight='bold')
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('plots/07_rapm_ridge_regression.png', dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight', facecolor='#0d1117')
plt.show()
```

The left panel shows the key insight: the model *deliberately* shrinks low-TOI players' estimates more than high-TOI players. This is the correct move — a player with 200 minutes of ice time has a far noisier RAPM estimate than one with 1,400 minutes, and the model reflects that uncertainty by pulling the estimate closer to league average. The right panel shows that at a moderate λ, RAPM does a good job recovering actual underlying talent — not perfect, but meaningfully better than raw observed statistics.

### From RAPM to WAR

The **Evolving Hockey WAR model** (Arsenault & Desjardins, 2019) builds on RAPM by:

1. Breaking the player effect into components: even-strength offense, even-strength defense, powerplay, penalty kill, penalties drawn/taken, faceoffs
2. Scaling each component from xG units to **Goals Above Replacement (GAR)**
3. Converting goals to wins using a league-wide goals-per-win factor (~5–6 goals per win)

$$
\text{WAR} = \frac{\text{GAR}_{\text{total}} }{\text{Goals Per Win}}
$$

For goalies, the equivalent is **Goals Saved Above Expected (GSAx)**:

$$
\text{GSAx} = xGA - GA
$$

A goalie with GSAx = +12 saved 12 more goals than an average goalie would have against the same shot profile. At ~5–6 goals per win, that's roughly 2 wins of goalie value above average.

### The WAR Era's Limits

WAR was a genuine leap — suddenly you could ask "is this defenseman worth his $6M contract?" with actual math behind the answer. But the model remained constrained by what the data could see: **everything measured was still shot-attempt derived**. A player's defensive positioning, his off-puck movement, his ability to win board battles and create separation — none of it registered unless it eventually showed up as a shot attempt. The map was better, but the territory was still mostly invisible.

## Generation 4 — The Tracking Era (2022–Present)

### The Problem Being Solved

> *"Hockey is 60 minutes of off-puck movement, defensive reads, and positioning decisions. Most of it never becomes a shot attempt."*

The NHL's partnership with optical player tracking technology — deployed at scale starting around 2021–22 — finally opened the door to the layer of hockey that had always been analytically invisible. Every player's position, speed, and movement is now tracked multiple times per second, and the puck itself carries a chip that logs its location and velocity.

### Zone Entries and Exits: The Bridge Metric

Even before full tracking arrived, manually-tracked **zone entry and exit data** (pioneered by Corey Sznajder's *All Three Zones* project starting around 2013) revealed something fundamental about how offense is actually generated.

### Tracking-Era Metrics

Full player and puck tracking has since enabled a new generation of measurements that previously existed only as coaching intuitions:

| Metric | What it measures | Technology required |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Puck speed at shot** | Velocity (mph) at release | Puck chip |
| **Rush shot flag** | Shot within 4 sec of zone entry | Event + tracking |
| **Pass before shot** | Cross-ice pass preceding attempt | Sportlogiq |
| **Defensive zone coverage** | % of time in correct position | Player tracking |
| **Separation on entry** | Skater speed vs nearest defender | Player tracking |
| **Expected Turnovers (xTO)** | Probability of puck loss on given play | ML on tracking data |

### What's Next: The AI Frontier

The most advanced work happening in NHL analytics departments right now — most of it proprietary — is pushing into territory that would have been science fiction a decade ago:

- **Graph neural networks** that model all 10 skaters simultaneously as a formation, not as individuals
- **Sequence models (LSTM / Transformer)** that compute xG based on the last 10–15 seconds of play, not just the shot itself
- **Off-puck movement valuation** using optical flow to assign credit for creating passing lanes and drawing defenders
- **Integrated contract models** that predict future performance and cap value from the full metric stack

## Conclusion: Why No Single Metric Is Ever Enough

The history of NHL analytics is not a story of one metric replacing another. It's a story of **layering** — each generation answering a question the previous one couldn't ask.

| The problem | The solution |
|-------------|-------------|
| +/− is too noisy | → Corsi: higher sample rate, real signal |
| Corsi ignores shot quality | → xG: probability-weighted shots |
| xG can't isolate individual skill | → WAR/RAPM: regression-based context adjustment |
| WAR can't see off-puck play | → Tracking: full player and puck data |

The modern analyst doesn't choose between these generations. They read all four simultaneously, the way a doctor synthesizes blood work, imaging, and clinical observation before making a diagnosis. Each layer confirms, challenges, or contextualizes the others.

Corsi isn't enough. But it was the insight that started everything — and understanding *why* it isn't enough is what makes every metric that came after it meaningful.
